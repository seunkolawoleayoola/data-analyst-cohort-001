## Practice: Cleaning data: duplicates, TRIM/PROPER, text-to-columns, date formats, and validation rules
**Completed:** 2026-07-17 | **Method:** SOLVE

> **Scenario:** Sector: Charity / NGO
> 
> Scenario: A fundraising coordinator exported 8 donor records from three sources: a web form, a paper signup sheet, and a legacy spreadsheet. Each has different formatting. Fix everything before personalised thank-you letters go out.
> 
> Sample donor data:
> 
> | DonorID | Name           | Email                  | Phone        | DonationDate | Amount |
> |---------|----------------|------------------------|--------------|--------------|--------|
> | D001    | ALICE MORGAN   | alice.morgan@email.com | 07700900001  | 12-Jan-2024  | £50.00 |
> | D002    |  bob harris    | BOB.HARRIS@EMAIL.COM   | 07700 900002 | 2024/02/03   | £25    |
> | D003    | Carol  White   | carol.white@email.com  | 07700900003  | 15 Mar 2024  | £100   |
> | D002    |  bob harris    | BOB.HARRIS@EMAIL.COM   | 07700 900002 | 2024/02/03   | £25    |
> | D004    | DAVID CHEN     | d.chen@email.com       | 07700900004  | 01/04/2024   | £75.00 |
> | D005    | eve   johnson  | eve.johnson@email.com  | 07700900005  | 5th May 2024 | £200   |
> | D006    | Frank PATEL    | frank.patel@email.com  | 07700900006  | 20-Jun-2024  | £30.00 |
> | D007    | Grace OBI      | grace.obi@email.com    | 07700900007  | 07/07/2024   | £150.00|
> 
> Six fixes to apply:
> 1. Flag D002 duplicate with COUNTIF — remove one row
> 2. Name: `=TRIM(PROPER(B2))` on all 7 remaining records
> 3. Email: `=LOWER(TRIM(C2))`
> 4. Phone: `=SUBSTITUTE(D2," ","")` — all should be 11 digits, no spaces
> 5. DonationDate: DATEVALUE to normalise all formats to date serial
> 6. Amount: `=VALUE(SUBSTITUTE(F2,"£",""))` — remove £ symbol, convert to number
> 
> Verification: Total donations after cleaning → £630. Average → £90. 7 unique records, all fields clean.
> 
> ## Hidden Tips
> 
> - Build from scratch, not from copy — close your learn-day notes before starting and reconstruct data cleaning: TRIM, PROPER, text-to-columns, dates, validation from memory; the errors you encounter now are ones you will not make at work under deadline pressure.
> - Verification numbers are non-negotiable — do not move on until your output matches the expected result exactly; a one-row off-by-one error is a real data error, not a rounding issue to ignore.
> - Name your file with the week and context — `week-03-practice.xlsx` stays findable six months from now; `exercise.xlsx` does not.
> - Write one sentence of interpretation before saving — after building your output, spend 2 minutes writing what the result tells a business; if you cannot write that sentence, you do not understand the output yet.
> 
> ## Interview Questions
> 
> Q1: An interviewer gives you a dataset and 20 minutes to explore it in Excel. What do you do first? → Check the shape (row count, column count), scan for obvious issues (blanks, unexpected data types, outliers in the first and last rows), understand what each column represents, and confirm the grain of the data (one row = one what?). Understanding before analysing is what separates junior from mid-level analysts.
> 
> Q2: Your output in Excel gives a different total than the expected value. How do you debug it? → I work backwards: check the source row count, check for hidden rows or filters, check for duplicates in the key column, check data types (text masquerading as numbers sums to zero), check the formula or query range. I document each check before trying the next.
